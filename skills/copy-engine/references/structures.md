# Copy Structures — Emotional Arc Patterns

These are structural shapes for a piece of copy — not tones, not frameworks
(PAS/AIDA are in frameworks.md). A structure defines the emotional arc:
what the reader feels first, second, third.

Structures are named. New structures can be invented per piece and added
here if they prove useful. Each structure must document its emotional arc,
its risk, and when to use it.

---

## MAR — Mirror → Acquit → Reframe
**The Therapist**

Arc: "I see you" → "It's not your fault" → "Here's what's actually going on"

1. Open by describing the reader's private experience so accurately they
   feel caught. Details must be specific and true — one wrong detail breaks it.
2. Acquit: name the guilt/shame they feel and immediately release it.
3. Reframe: reveal what's actually happening underneath.

Feels like: a therapist who describes your week back to you, then tells you
what's actually going on.

Best for: emotionally isolated readers. Sections that need intimacy. When
the reader's problem is loneliness or self-blame.

Risk: too slow if the mirror lingers. Presumptuous if details don't land.
The most intimate structure — only use when you have specific, verified
emotional material from Step 3.

Energy: warm anger. Campfire. "We're in this together."

---

## GAV — Guilt → Absolve → Villain
**The Defense Lawyer**

Arc: Gut punch → immediate relief → redirect anger to the real culprit

1. Open by naming the shame/guilt directly. "You've spent weeks on this.
   Nothing works. You're wondering if you're the problem."
2. IMMEDIATELY absolve: "You're not the problem."
3. Indict the real villain — name names. Tools, companies, industry norms.

Feels like: a lawyer who says "my client is innocent — here's who actually
did it."

Best for: readers who've been burned by tools/companies. When there's a
clear villain to name. Confrontational copy. Manifesto energy.

Risk: accusation too harsh → reader bounces before the absolving. Naming
competitors can feel petty if not earned. Must be righteous, not bitter.

Energy: hot anger. Courtroom. "Someone should answer for this."

---

## SYM — Symptoms → Yield → Mechanism
**The Doctor**

Arc: Stack recognizable symptoms → reveal they're all one problem → show
the structural fix

1. Stack 3-5 symptoms the reader will recognize. Short, blunt, specific.
   Each one lands a small "yes, that's me." They accumulate weight.
2. The turn: "These feel like five different problems. They're one."
3. Reveal the single underlying cause + the mechanism that fixes it.

Feels like: a doctor who lists your unrelated symptoms and tells you
they're all caused by one thing.

Best for: analytical readers. When you have multiple concrete symptoms to
stack. When the "click" of understanding is the emotional payoff.

Risk: feels like a listicle if symptoms aren't sharp enough. Clinical if
it lacks warmth. Requires very specific, true symptoms from Step 3.

Energy: cold anger. Lab report. "Here's the evidence."

## Adding new structures

When a co-thinking session produces a new structural shape:

1. Name it (3-letter code preferred)
2. Define the arc in 3 steps
3. Write the "feels like" metaphor
4. Note best-for and risk
5. Add it here

A structure earns its place by being used successfully on real copy.
Delete structures that never get reused.
