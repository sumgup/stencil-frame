# Failure Modes — Stencil + Frame copy reference

This document is reference material for the copy skill (`SKILL.md`). It supplies
the *ammunition* the core frameworks require: documented positioning failure
modes, the symptom language founders actually use, and the misdiagnosis each
mode hides behind. Use it before selecting a framework — identify which failure
mode the reader is living in, then write to that.

Status: modes 2 and 5 are locked-candidate quality (landing page priority).
Modes 1, 3, 4, 6 are complete first-pass drafts, open for section-by-section
locking.

---

## Standing principle — the misdiagnosis insight

**Positioning failure is systematically misdiagnosed.** Post-mortems attribute
failure to cash, churn, competition, or "no market need." Almost no founder
autopsy says "we failed because of positioning." The disease hides inside the
symptoms it produces.

The strongest available data makes this explicit: CB Insights' 2024 analysis of
431 VC-backed shutdowns found 70% "ran out of capital" — and names that the
final symptom, not the root cause. Poor product-market fit (43%), bad timing
(29%), and broken unit economics (19%) are the underlying diseases. Positioning
failure lives un-itemized inside "product-market fit" — the product was often
fine; nobody could tell who it was for or why it was different.

**Copy rule (non-negotiable):** never invent a percentage for positioning
failure. There is no "X% of brands fail from bad positioning" statistic and
fabricating one would violate the Ogilvy specificity standard and brand.md's
honesty register. The un-measurability *is* the claim. The adjacent figures
(43% PMF, 70% capital-as-symptom) may be cited as adjacent evidence, correctly
attributed, never as a positioning-failure rate.

**Why this is a headline mechanism:** the target reader (per brand.md) is
problem-aware but *wrongly diagnosed*. They have already named a symptom —
"we need better content," "our churn is too high," "the logo feels off." Copy
that merely names pain tells them what they know. Copy that names the
**misdiagnosis** reframes what they know. This is a Schwartz awareness-level
move almost no competitor can make, because it requires this research
underneath it. Every headline built on it must trace to a mode below —
auditability dogfooded.

## Mode 1 — Undifferentiated (first-pass draft)

**Name:** The brand says nothing a competitor couldn't say.

**Documented case:** The generic-buzzword pattern — "innovative," "trusted,"
"quality-driven" — appears across positioning post-mortems as the most common
self-inflicted wound. When every cereal box says "Tasty and Healthy," no box
gets picked. (See also the copy-engine buzzword test: if a word could appear
in any competitor's copy unchanged, it does no work.)

**How the founder experiences it:** "We keep rewriting the About page and it
still sounds like everyone else. We've A/B-tested four taglines and none of
them moved anything."

**The misdiagnosis:** "Our copywriting isn't good enough." The founder hires a
better writer, buys a course, tries a new tone. The words weren't the problem;
there was no difference for the words to carry.

**Stencil act:** Act 2 (What makes us different — Johnson's forced
concentration on true differentials). No copy technique fixes an empty
answer to that question.

**Copy applications:** Halbert frustration-naming (the rewrite loop is the
nameable frustration); Ogilvy specificity as the visible antidote — the copy
itself must demonstrate the cure by being un-swappable.

## Mode 2 — Everyone-is-the-audience (landing page priority)

**Name:** Casting the net so wide it catches no one.

**Documented case:** The pattern is documented across positioning literature as
the most seductive failure: trying to please everyone produces bland messaging
that appeals to no one; the brand seems aimless, and prospective buyers can't
tell what it stands for or why it should matter to them. WW's move from weight
loss to broad "wellness" is the flagship case — it abandoned a distinct,
owned position to compete against established lifestyle brands with no clear
edge, and membership declined. The repositioning resonated with neither the
existing audience nor the new one. Muji is the instructive inverse: an
aesthetic so specific that its target market is self-selecting — deliberate
exclusion as a positioning act.

**How the founder experiences it:** "Our engagement is fine but nothing
converts. People say 'looks great!' and never buy. When someone asks what we
do, I give a different answer depending on who's asking — and I've started to
notice that."

**The misdiagnosis:** "We need to reach more people." The founder widens
targeting, adds content pillars, softens the message to avoid excluding
anyone — which is the disease presenting as its own cure. Reach was never the
constraint; recognition was.

**Stencil act:** Act 1 (audience interrogation) — and specifically the
push-back mechanic: Stencil is the tool that rejects "everyone who values
quality" as an answer and asks again.

**Copy applications:**
- Schwartz opener from the *symptom the founder can already see*: the
  different-answer-depending-on-who's-asking moment. That is the recognizable
  tension for a problem-aware reader.
- Halbert: the frustration is not "no audience," it's "polite audience,
  no buyers" — name that exactly.
- Self-implicating register (Variant B compatible): the landing page can admit
  the temptation — everyone building anything has softened a sentence to avoid
  excluding someone.
- Off-limits: imagistic register (reserved per brand.md).

## Mode 3 — Identity abandonment (first-pass draft)

**Name:** Trading an owned position for a borrowed one.

**Documented case:** WW again (it straddles modes 2 and 3 — the abandonment
*produced* the everyone-audience). Cosmopolitan's yogurt is the purer case:
readers trusted Cosmo on health, sex, and professional life, not dairy; the
product died in 18 months. The failure wasn't research — it was departure from
what the brand was *for*.

**How the founder experiences it:** "The new direction feels bigger, more
ambitious. But our oldest customers have gone quiet, and the new ones we
imagined haven't shown up."

**The misdiagnosis:** "We outgrew our niche." Sometimes true — but the
documented pattern is that transitions fail when customers don't understand
*why* you're changing. The problem isn't the move; it's the missing bridge
from the existing position.

**Stencil act:** Act 0 (why we're here) — the identity anchor that makes
expansion legible instead of arbitrary. Frame's traceability makes the bridge
visible in every asset.

**Copy applications:** credibility-category techniques; Ogilvy reason-why for
any change narrative.

## Mode 4 — Promise-reality gap (first-pass draft)

**Name:** Marketing writes checks the product can't cash.

**Documented case:** Documented as an internal-misalignment pattern: marketing
shoots for the stars, product is an MVP that can't deliver, the customer
experience is jarring. Trust burns at the exact moment of first contact.

**How the founder experiences it:** "Signups are decent, activation is a
cliff. People arrive expecting something we haven't built yet."

**The misdiagnosis:** "Onboarding needs work." Sometimes. But if the copy
promised a different product, no onboarding flow closes that gap.

**Stencil act:** the brand.md derivation chain itself — copy generated *from*
the positioning spec cannot outrun what the spec claims. Auditability as a
structural guard against aspirational drift.

**Copy applications:** the copy-engine self-check (steps 3–5, non-skippable)
is the productized version of this mode's cure — cite it as proof, never as
decoration.

## Mode 5 — Cosmetic fix for structural problem / the false autopsy (landing page priority)

**Name:** Rebranding the symptom while the disease proceeds.

**Documented case:** Two canonical, opposite-direction cases.
*RadioShack* (2009–2017): renamed to "The Shack," changed the logo three
times, repositioned as a mobile retailer — none of it worked because the
underlying problem was irrelevant product selection. Two bankruptcies. The
rebrands were expensive theater that delayed the real transformation.
*Yahoo* (2013): a 30-day logo spectacle that addressed none of the actual
problems — declining relevance, product confusion, talent exodus. Yahoo had a
positioning problem, not a logo problem; the fix was applied one layer too
shallow. Crystal Pepsi is the launch-side sibling: a genuinely unique product
nobody wanted, because nobody minded that cola was brown in the first place —
a gap that was never a gap. The differentiator existed; it just didn't matter.

**The discriminator this mode teaches:** every failure story must be
interrogated with one question — *was this a story problem or a substance
problem?* Only story problems are positioning failures. RadioShack's was
substance (no positioning could save the product mix). Yahoo's was closer to
positioning-neglected-until-fatal. Crystal Pepsi's was positioning proper:
differentiation without relevance. This is the two-sided error brand.md
already names — gap without identity, identity without gap.

**How the founder experiences it:** "We refreshed the logo, redid the site,
rewrote the deck. It looked better. The numbers didn't move. So now we're
talking about refreshing it again."

**The misdiagnosis:** the surface loop itself — the founder keeps adjusting
what's visible because the invisible layer has no handle to grab. This is
brand.md's core audience tension verbatim: founders who sense their visuals
aren't earned but lack language for why, so they keep adjusting the surface.

**Stencil act:** the whole product is this mode's answer — Act 0 before
assets, positioning before logo, "before the logo, there's the reason."

**Copy applications:**
- The false-autopsy frame: "ran out of cash" is where the story *ends*, not
  where it starts. Structurally parallel to the NFX blood-loss line but must
  be written fresh, never borrowed.
- Headline shapes this mode licenses (mechanisms, not candidates):
  symptom-vs-disease ("You don't have a content problem"), the surface loop
  named ("The third redesign won't fix it either"), Crystal Pepsi as parable
  ("Nobody minded that cola was brown").
- Confrontational register (Variant A compatible); D1 Strike-Through is the
  natural visual pairing — the wrong diagnosis literally struck through.
- Discipline: when using Crystal Pepsi/RadioShack/Yahoo, one case per copy
  block maximum. Case-study pileups read as content marketing, not
  conviction.

## Mode 6 — Equity vandalism (first-pass draft)

**Name:** Destroying recognition the brand already owned.

**Documented case:** Gap's 2010 logo lasted six days; the failure wasn't the
design but changing an iconic element without understanding its emotional
significance to the people who loved it. Tropicana's redesign sits in the same
file. Pattern across cases: strong recognition thrown away for "fresh," then
months spent recovering what was already owned.

**How the founder experiences it:** (least relevant to Stencil's solo-founder
audience pre-launch; most relevant to positioning *individuals* — an artist or
account that pivots aesthetic and sheds the audience that recognized them.)

**The misdiagnosis:** "It was looking dated." Recognition and datedness are
different properties; only one of them converts.

**Stencil act:** Act 3+ visual derivation — change is legitimate when it
traces to a positioning change; arbitrary when it doesn't.

**Copy applications:** secondary for the landing page; primary for future
artist/creator-facing copy (see universality note).

## Universality note — positioning beyond companies

Johnson deliberately reduces positioning to "what makes us different?" — a
question an illustrator, a newsletter, or an Instagram account can answer as
legitimately as a corporation. Two adaptations when the entity is an
individual: the market is *attention*, not revenue (gap maps drawn against
competing claims on the same audience's attention); and identity carries
proportionally more weight — an artist positioned purely on a market gap
produces content, not a body of work. The gap-without-identity error is
*more* fatal for individuals. This widens Stencil's addressable audience and
strengthens the core thesis; failing Instagram accounts are almost always
Mode 1 or Mode 2.
