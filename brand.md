# Stencil + Frame

## Identity

**Who** — Stencil + Frame is an open-source, AI-agnostic brand engine built on
the thesis that positioning is the hero: soul-first brand identity precedes
visual design or content generation. Two components: Stencil (guided brand
discovery — positioning, purpose, values, voice) and Frame (downstream content
generation that consumes what Stencil produces).

**Why** — Founders are handed logo generators and archetype quizzes when what
they actually need is a reason to exist. Every chapter of the system derives
from Chapter 1 (positioning) — that auditability is the thing no competitor
does.

## Positioning

**Purpose** — Stencil + Frame exists because founders are handed shortcuts to
visuals when what they need is a forced, honest answer to "why are we here."

**Practice** — A guided, multi-step discovery (Stencil) produces a structured,
machine-readable `brand.md`. Voice, visual direction, and content (Frame) are
generated *from* that file, never independently of it.

**Difference** — Most tools either extract a voice from content you already
wrote (Jasper, Copy.ai) or hand you a template filled in one AI pass
(thebrand.md's interview, Looka's archetype quiz). Stencil is a guided,
interrogative process — willing to reject a weak answer and ask again — that
treats the resulting positioning document as the actual deliverable, not a
formality before the "real" output of logos and copy. The experience of being
asked is itself the differentiator.

**Audience** — Solo and early-stage founders who have a real reason to exist
but haven't been forced to articulate it, and are one Canva template away
from a brand that looks fine and means nothing.

**Values** — Derivation over decoration. Hand-made before automated.
Positioning as the hero, not a checkbox before the real work.

**Personality** — Authoritative, but only because it's been wrong before.
Stencil + Frame speaks with conviction, not because it has all the answers,
but because it asked itself the hard question first and knows exactly how
much that cost. Warmth shows up as honesty about that cost, not as
reassurance — it comforts by being truthful, never by softening the point.

**Personality tension** — Confident enough to state a position; honest enough
to admit the position was hard-won, not obvious from the start.

**Rejects** — Auto-generated identity from a single AI pass. Archetype quizzes
that assign a pre-made personality instead of deriving one. Voice or visuals
extracted from content written before the brand's reason for existing was
ever articulated. Treating positioning as a checkbox before logos and copy.
Any process a founder can complete without being made to sit with a hard
question.

**Belief shift** — From "a brand tool should be fast and give me an answer" to
"a brand tool should be slow enough to make me find the true answer myself."

**Audience tension** — Founders know their visuals don't feel earned, but
lack language for why — so they keep tweaking fonts and colors instead of the
thing underneath, because the tool that would force the harder conversation
doesn't exist yet.

## Positioning axes (competitive map reference)

Two axes locate Stencil + Frame against the field. Full competitive research:
`Stencil_and_Frame__Competitive_Landscape_for_an_Open-Source__Positioning-First_Brand_Engine.md`

**Axis 1 — Extractive ↔ Generative from positioning**
Does the tool assume a brand already exists and pull a voice out of it
(Jasper, Copy.ai, brandmd token-scraper), or does it force positioning to be
articulated before anything is produced? Stencil + Frame sits at the
generative extreme.

**Axis 2 — Production-first ↔ Strategy-first**
Is the primary deliverable an asset (logo, post, page — Canva, Looka), or is
the structured positioning document itself the deliverable, with visuals/copy
explicitly downstream? Stencil + Frame sits at the strategy-first extreme.

**Axis 3 — Self-serve/one-shot ↔ Guided/interrogative**
This is the axis that separates Stencil + Frame from its nearest neighbor,
thebrand.md (open MIT standard, real Positioning/Strategy fields, but filled
via a single AI-driven interview). Stencil + Frame is a multi-step, six
question discovery (Johnson's *Branding in Five and a Half Steps* as
reference, not rigid structure) willing to push back on a weak answer
("try saying that without any industry words at all") rather than accept the
first response.

**Nearest competitor** — thebrand.md (caiopizzol/brand.md, MIT licensed).
Same file-in-project-root philosophy, same "AGENTS.md for brand" framing.
Differs on depth: one-pass AI interview vs. guided multi-step interrogation,
and on framing: enterprise/multi-product brand architecture vs. solo-founder
self-discovery.

## Voice

**Philosophy** — Stencil + Frame writes the way it thinks: unwilling to skip
the hard question for a faster answer. Confident enough to state a position
plainly, honest enough to admit when it doesn't fully know. Says the true
thing about the industry, then doesn't flinch from saying it again.

**Do's**
1. State the claim plainly, no hedging. ("Positioning is the hero" — not
   "we believe positioning might be important.")
2. Undercut decoration by naming it specifically. ("Jazzy colors," "boxes in
   nice fonts" — not "superficial branding.")
3. Admit uncertainty as earned history, not present-tense hedging. Not "we
   might be wrong" — but "we got this wrong first, which is how we know."
   The crack is in the past, the authority is in the present.
4. Use one image, once, when it does more work than an argument. (Rare,
   protected — not a habit.)
5. Let the joke stay flat. No wink, no exclamation mark selling the joke.

**Don'ts**
1. Don't explain the joke. If it needs a follow-up clarifying the irony, cut it.
2. Don't use industry jargon uncritically. ("Archetype," "brand kit,"
   "aesthetic" — usable only when being examined or rejected.)
3. Don't soften a claim with qualifiers. No "we think," "sort of," "in our
   opinion."
4. Don't reach for the poetic/imagistic move more than once per piece.
5. Don't punch down or mock the reader's doubt or inexperience. Dark humor
   targets the industry's dishonesty, never the founder reading this.

**Reference moves**
1. *The flat-delivery dark joke* — state something that should alarm you, in
   a neutral tone, no punctuation doing the work. ("We've been doing branding
   wrong, but let's be silent about it.")
2. *The concrete-image rejection* — reject something by naming its specific,
   visual detail rather than an abstract category. ("Boxes in jazzy colors,"
   not "shallow branding.")
3. *The self-implicating aside* — mid-claim, admit the claim could be
   self-serving or uncertain, which makes it land harder.

**Anti-examples**
1. "We're passionate about helping brands unlock their true potential through
   innovative AI-powered solutions." — hedged, buzzword-driven, no stakes.
2. "OMG we just built something SO exciting — you're going to LOVE what's
   coming!!" — performs enthusiasm instead of earning trust.
3. "Branding can feel scary sometimes, but don't worry, we're here to make it
   fun and easy for you!" — softens the hard question instead of sitting
   with it.

## Guardrails

If it sounds like a pitch deck, rewrite it. If a sentence needs an
exclamation mark to feel exciting, the sentence isn't doing its job. If a
joke needs explaining, cut it. Never assign a personality (archetype-style)
instead of deriving one.
